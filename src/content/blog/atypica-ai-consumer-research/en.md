A solo operator opens Atypica, types a product description and a target segment, and gets a 30-page consumer-insight report in 30 minutes. A traditional research firm would quote 5,000-15,000 USD and 2-4 weeks for the same deliverable. The case argues that this gap will halve the mid-tier research industry within a year. Numbers, math, and where the claim strains.

## The cost rewrite in plain numbers

```
Traditional consumer-insight engagement (mid-tier firm):
  Cost:    USD 5,000 - 15,000 per project
  Cycle:   2 - 4 weeks
  Output:  30-50 page report
  Pool:    companies with a research budget

Atypica-assisted solo run:
  Cost:    API subscription (around 50-200 USD/month) + operator time
  Cycle:   30 minutes
  Output:  comparable screening report
  Pool:    anyone with a question
```

The shift is roughly 100x on cost, 100x on cycle, and the buyer pool expands by an order of magnitude. The deliverable is not the same depth as a primary-research study with 800 survey respondents — it is closer to a screening report. But the screening tier used to be the mid-tier firm's cash cow, and that is exactly what gets eaten.

## Who actually buys this, and at what price

Three buyer pools, sorted by what they used to do:

| Buyer | Old behavior | New behavior | Realistic price |
|---|---|---|---|
| Solo founder | Skipped the research step | Pays a solo operator for a 30-min Atypica deliverable | 200-500 USD |
| Growth marketer | Quarterly insight check from a firm | Runs the check weekly, in-house | Cost is the operator's salary, not a budget line |
| Boutique consultancy | Did the screening study themselves at 5K USD | Uses Atypica to triage; escalates only high-stakes projects | Saves 50-70% of mid-tier research spend |

The first row is the volume play. The original case argues that this is exactly where the addressable market expands — buyers who used to skip the step now do it for 200-500 USD instead of 5,000 USD.

## The mid-tier research firm math

A mid-tier firm in 2026 typically runs:
- 5-15 projects per quarter at 5-15K USD
- 1-3 senior consultants per project, plus analyst bench time
- The screening study tier is what keeps the bench billable

If half of the screening study volume moves to Atypica-style solo operators at one-tenth the price, the firm's revenue line drops 50% but the cost line drops only 20% (the senior consultants are still needed for the high-end projects). The bench thins first. The case is right that "traditional firms have to downsize" — the question is which tier of firm survives. The high-end primary-research work (regulated markets, qualitative panels) is durable; the volume-screening tier is the one that goes.

## The indie operator's actual workflow

A realistic freelance pattern, per the case:

1. **Free preview**: the operator runs a 5-minute Atypica pass and sends the prospect a one-page summary. No charge. This collapses the trust barrier the same way the AI website case does — the buyer sees a deliverable before signing a check.
2. **200-500 USD paid deliverable**: the operator polishes the Atypica output, adds one human interpretation pass, and ships a 15-25 page report. This is the price the buyer will pay because it is still 10-50x cheaper than a firm.
3. **Optional add-on**: for buyers who want a real qualitative round, the operator subcontracts 5-10 interviews at 50-100 USD each, paid out of the engagement fee. The operator's margin on the add-on is thin.

The math: 30 minutes of operator time, 200-500 USD revenue, no panel, no contract. The cost is the API subscription, which is amortized across all clients.

## What this case does not cover

- **Primary research depth.** Atypica is a screening tool. It does not replace 800-respondent surveys, ethnography, or regulated market-access work. The "30 minutes" headline assumes a clear brief — vague questions still need a human to translate before the tool can answer.
- **Buyer-side trust.** Many enterprise buyers still want a named firm on the cover page for regulatory or board-level reasons. The solo operator path is real for the volume tier, but the high-end engagements still go to firms with brands.
- **Liability.** If an Atypica-screened recommendation leads to a product launch that fails, the firm is shielded by contract. The solo operator carries that risk personally. Insurance, contracts, and a clear scope of work matter here.
- **Subscription economics.** The case assumes the operator keeps an Atypica-style API subscription continuously. A solo operator who uses the tool only for client work pays a fixed cost regardless of client volume, which means a slow month hits margin harder than it would at a firm with salaried bench.

## Take-away

The volume tier of consumer research is moving from "firm + project" to "solo operator + 30 minutes." The mid-tier firms lose the bench, not the work. The buyer pool expands by 10x because the entry price drops from 5,000 USD to 200-500 USD. Solo operators who frame themselves as "the research step you used to skip" have a real play. The case's "30 minutes" claim is real for screening, not for primary research; the latter is durable.
