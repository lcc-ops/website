A Chinese operator curates 5 open-source AI side-hustle playbooks on GitHub. The collection covers AI tools, SaaS, self-media, API aggregation, overseas projects, AI-content production, AI-image services, and AI-drama production. The case is not a single playbook — it is a meta-read of the recurring shape of 2026 AI side-hustle playbooks. The recurring shape: every playbook ships a workflow diagram, a tool list, and a 'revenue curve' that is suspiciously steep. The unit-economics section is missing in 4 of 5. The meta-lesson: the playbooks ship the upside, skip the gap, and the gap is where most readers lose money. Below is the recurring shape, the unit-economics gap, and four gaps the case does not address.

## The recurring shape across all 5 playbooks

```
Playbook section              Shipped?    Notes
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Workflow diagram              yes         input → AI → output → distribution
Tool list                     yes         model providers, automation, payment
Revenue curve                 yes         linear growth, no plateau
Success story (third-party)   yes         not the author's own revenue
CAC section                   no          ──
Conversion rate section       no          ──
Refund / chargeback section   no          ──
Decay / tail section          no          ──
Median outcome section        no          ──
Failure-mode section          no          ──
```

The shape is consistent across all 5 playbooks. The structural argument is not that the playbooks are wrong — it is that they are incomplete. The playbooks ship the upside and skip the gap. The gap is where the unit-economics risk lives.

## The unit-economics gap

```
Playbook claims:                      Reality (operator-side):
- Linear growth                      - 70% of operators see 0–$1K in 90 days
- AI does the work                   - AI does 60–80% of work; human does
                                        prompt design, quality control,
                                        distribution
- Tools are listed                    - Tools are listed; pricing and
                                        renewal are not
- Success story is cited              - Median outcome is not cited
- No CAC mentioned                   - CAC is the dominant cost
- No conversion rate mentioned       - Conversion rate is 1–5% from free
                                        to paid
- No refund rate mentioned           - Refund rate is 5–15%
- No decay mentioned                 - Algorithm change decays revenue
                                        in month 3–6
```

The gap is structural. The playbooks assume a frictionless distribution channel; the reality is that distribution is the bottleneck. The playbooks assume a free or near-free tool stack; the reality is that the tool stack has compounding monthly costs that add up to $200–$2,000/month at scale.

## Why the playbooks work for the top 5–10%, not the median

The playbooks work for operators who already have:
- a distribution channel (existing audience, paid traffic, or a content engine)
- a willingness to iterate on prompt design and quality control
- the financial runway to absorb 3–6 months of negative cash flow

For these operators, the playbook is a starting template that they adapt and execute. For the median reader — who has none of the above — the playbook is a recipe that produces a few weeks of activity followed by abandonment.

The structural argument is: the playbooks are written for the operator, not for the reader. The reader who follows a playbook verbatim loses money in the gap. The reader who treats the playbook as a starting template and fills in the unit-economics gap has a shot at the upside.

## What the case does not cover

Four gaps.

1. **Author revenue.** The playbooks do not name the author's own revenue, only curated third-party success stories. The author's own outcome is the central evidence; without it, the playbooks are theoretical.
2. **Median outcome.** No median outcome is reported; only the top-of-distribution outcomes are. A 'success' rate of 1–5% is plausible but unstated.
3. **Failure modes.** Failed playbooks are not collected; only working playbooks are. The survival bias inflates the implied success rate.
4. **The structural argument that playbooks work for the top 5–10% of operators and do not work for the median reader is not made in the case.** The case frames the collection as a positive resource; the gap is the unstated part.

## Take-away

The case is not '5 AI side-hustle playbooks on GitHub'. The case is: the 2026 AI side-hustle playbook ecosystem ships the upside and skips the unit-economics gap. The playbooks work for the top 5–10% of operators who already have distribution; they do not work for the median reader. The gap is where most readers lose money.

For most readers approaching these playbooks, the bottom line is: read the playbook as a starting template, not a recipe. Fill in the unit-economics gap before you ship the workflow. The upside is real for operators with distribution; the upside is theoretical for everyone else.
