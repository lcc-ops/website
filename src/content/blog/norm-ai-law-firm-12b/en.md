A case profiles Norm AI, founded by John Nay in July 2023. Series C: $120M raised at a $1.2B valuation. Total funding: $260M+. Customers manage over $30T in assets — Blackstone, Bain Capital, Vanguard, Citi, New York Life. The case lands not on the technology but on the structure: Norm does not sell a legal-software product. Norm runs a law firm called Norm Law. AI agents do the legal work (contract review, regulatory analysis, compliance checks); human senior lawyers supervise, calibrate, correct. The customer engages Norm Law as outside counsel. The deliverable is legal advice, not software output. The structural claim is the billing model — a firm bills by the matter, a tool bills by the seat. Below is the structural framing, the per-customer math, and four gaps the case does not address.

## The structural frame

```
Customer need:   "Review 10,000 contracts for a specific clause"
   ↓
Tool shape:      "Subscribe to our legal-AI tool, your team uses it"
   - pricing:     per-seat
   - ceiling:     customer's legal headcount
   - deliverable: software output, customer reviews
   ↓
Firm shape:      "Engage Norm Law to review 10,000 contracts"
   - pricing:     per-matter / fixed fee
   - ceiling:     customer's legal workload
   - deliverable: legal advice, Norm stamps it
```

The case is not 'Norm has better AI'. The case is: Norm operates a firm, not a tool. A firm's ceiling is the customer's workload; a tool's ceiling is the customer's headcount. For an enterprise customer whose legal workload is much larger than its legal headcount, the firm frame is the higher-ceiling revenue model.

## The customer roster

| Customer | AUM / assets managed | Implied deal size (rough) |
|---|---|---|
| Blackstone | ~$1.1T AUM | $2M–$10M/year |
| Bain Capital | ~$185B AUM | $1M–$5M/year |
| Vanguard | ~$8T AUM | $5M–$20M/year |
| Citi | ~$2.4T assets | $5M–$20M/year |
| New York Life | ~$700B assets | $2M–$10M/year |
| Total | $30T+ | implied $20M–$100M/year book |

The case does not name per-customer revenue. The implied range is consistent with a $50M–$150M ARR company. The customer concentration is the unstated variable — a small number of Fortune-class contracts likely dominate the book.

## Unit economics

```
Big-law billable benchmark:     $500–$2,500/hour
Norm Law billing rate:          $200–$1,000/hour equivalent (estimated)
Senior lawyer cost:             $400–$800/hour fully loaded
AI agent cost:                  $5–$50/hour equivalent (model + review)
Supervision ratio (assumed):     1 senior lawyer per 3–10 agents
Effective gross margin:         50–80% (firm shape, asset-light)
```

The case does not give the supervision ratio, which is the central unit-economics variable. A ratio of 1 senior lawyer per 10 agents is software-grade margin (80%+). A ratio of 1 per 2 is firm-grade margin (40–50%). The difference between the two is a 2x multiple on the company.

## Why 'firm' is the right frame in 2026

The structural argument is the same as Cognition's: the firm frame removes the per-seat revenue ceiling. The market for legal-software seats is competitive (Harvey, CoCounsel, Spellbook). The market for 'AI-staffed law firm capacity' is much less competitive, because most legal-AI vendors do not want to take on the unauthorized-practice-of-law risk that the firm frame requires.

The firm frame is also the only frame that justifies the $1.2B valuation. A per-seat legal-AI tool at $50M ARR would value at $250M–$500M. A law firm at $100M ARR with firm-shape margins would value at $1B–$2B. The firm frame is the one that supports the multiple.

## What the case does not cover

Four gaps.

1. **The leverage ratio.** How many agents per senior lawyer. This is the central unit-economics variable and is unstated. A 1:10 ratio is software-grade; a 1:2 ratio is firm-grade. The two cases have very different margins and multiples.
2. **The fee structure.** Fixed-fee vs hourly. A firm that bills hourly is a cost-plus business; a firm that bills fixed-fee per matter is a value-based business. The case does not name the structure.
3. **Malpractice exposure.** If an AI agent gives wrong legal advice, who carries the malpractice tail. The firm frame implies Norm carries it; the case does not address how the tail is underwritten.
4. **The revenue per customer.** The customer roster is named but the dollar value per customer is not. A Blackstone contract at $500K/year is a very different business from a Blackstone contract at $5M/year. The implied range is wide.

## Take-away

The case is not 'Norm's AI is better at legal work'. The case is: Norm operates a law firm, not a tool. A firm bills by the matter; a tool bills by the seat. The firm frame removes the per-seat ceiling. For an enterprise customer whose legal workload is much larger than its legal headcount, the firm frame is the higher-ceiling revenue model. The customer roster — $30T in managed assets — is the structural evidence.

For most operators building AI products in 2026, the bottom line is: stop competing on per-seat legal-AI tools. Operate a firm. The firm frame removes the per-seat ceiling, supports a higher multiple, and competes in a less crowded market — at the cost of taking on the regulatory burden of being a firm.
