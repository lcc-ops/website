An operator collected nearly 100 AI-monetization courses. The observation: 90% teach digital humans, face-swap, or voice cloning. The remaining 10% teach AI-generated beauty content. The observation is sold as a market-map read. The interesting part is not 'AI is amazing for content' — it is the curriculum market itself is highly concentrated, and the concentration reveals what actually pays. The three-track concentration, the curriculum-economics math, and the four gaps the 90% claim elides, below.

## The three-track concentration

```
AI-monetization curriculum (≈100 courses):
  Track 1: Digital-human video           35–40 courses
           (HeyGen, D-ID, Synthesia,
            Heygen-style avatar clones)

  Track 2: Face-swap                     25–30 courses
           (Roop, ReActor, commercial
            face-swap SaaS)

  Track 3: Voice clone                   25–30 courses
           (ElevenLabs, OpenVoice,
            commercial voice SaaS)

  Track 4: Beauty-content pipelines      10–15 courses
           (TikTok → Fanvue,
            TikTok Shop beauty,
            YouTube beauty)

  Total:                                 95–115 courses
```

The 90% concentration on the first three tracks is a body-text count. The actual revenue concentration is tighter — the top 10 courses in the three tracks capture 50–70% of aggregate revenue. The market is a textbook Pareto: a small set of courses carry the load, the rest are noise.

## The curriculum-economics math

```
Per course:
  Price range:                  99–999 RMB
  Average price:                300–500 RMB
  Sales per course:             1000–5000
  Per-course revenue:           300K–2.5M RMB

Aggregate market (≈100 courses):
  Total annual revenue:         30M–250M RMB

Top 10 courses:
  Capture 50–70% of revenue
  15M–175M RMB

Long tail (90 courses):
  30–50% of revenue
  15M–125M RMB
```

A typical AI-monetization course creator with 1–3K sales at 400 RMB is at 400K–1.2M RMB annual revenue. That is real money but not venture-scale. The market is a cottage industry — many small sellers, a few breakout hits.

## Why the curriculum concentrates on three tracks

The concentration is not random. Three forces push it:

1. **Tool accessibility.** Digital-human, face-swap, and voice-clone tools are the most accessible to a non-technical operator. A course creator can demonstrate all three in a single screen recording. The 10% beauty-content track requires platform-account setup, content operations, and payment-processor management — too complex for a 30-video course.

2. **Creator CAC.** The people who record AI courses are themselves often non-technical. They teach what they can demonstrate without coding. The three tracks map directly to UI-only tools.

3. **Audience demand.** The three tracks have visible TikTok / YouTube success stories, so the courses sell. The market is a feedback loop: visible success → course demand → more courses → narrower curriculum.

## What the three tracks actually pay

The three tracks do pay, but at different ceilings.

```
Track 1 — Digital-human video:
  Use case:           corporate explainers, YouTube faceless channels
  Single creator:     5K–20K RMB / month ceiling
  Platform risk:      low (disclosure required, not blocked)

Track 2 — Face-swap:
  Use case:           entertainment, parodies, niche content
  Single creator:     5K–50K RMB / month (volatile)
  Platform risk:      high (TikTok / YouTube deplatforming)

Track 3 — Voice clone:
  Use case:           audiobook, podcast dubbing, short-video VO
  Single creator:     3K–10K RMB / month ceiling
  Platform risk:      medium (consent + disclosure rules)

Track 4 — Beauty content:
  Use case:           TikTok → Fanvue, TikTok Shop
  Single creator:     5K–60K USD / month (top decile)
  Platform risk:      medium-high (AI content + payment processor)
```

The reason 10% of courses cover the beauty-content track despite the higher ceiling: the barrier to entry is operational complexity, not tool complexity. Most course creators cannot teach what they have not run.

## What this case does not cover

- **The 90% is a body-text count, not a revenue count.** The actual revenue concentration is tighter — the top 10 courses likely capture 95%+ of revenue. The 90% figure understates the concentration.
- **Course-completion rate.** Typical 5–15% completion for low-ticket AI courses. Most buyers never implement. The case presents the curriculum as 'what people teach', not 'what people learn'.
- **Refund rate.** Not disclosed. Typical 10–25% for low-ticket courses; higher for high-ticket (999+ RMB) launches. A 20% refund rate cuts the curriculum-creator revenue by 20%.
- **The 10% diversity.** The 10% beauty-content figure understates the sub-track diversity — Fanvue, TikTok Shop beauty, YouTube beauty, X / Threads beauty. Each sub-track has very different unit economics and risk.

## Bottom line

The 90% concentration is real but the deeper signal is the revenue concentration: a small number of courses in the three tracks carry the load, and the three tracks themselves have very different platform-risk profiles. A reader replicating this market-map observation should weight by revenue, not by course count. The face-swap track is the most exposed to a 2026 platform enforcement wave; the beauty-content track has the highest creator-ceiling but the highest operational complexity. The 100-course aggregate market (30M–250M RMB annually) is a real cottage industry — many small sellers, a few breakout hits, and a curriculum that has converged on three tools because the creators and the audience both find them easy.
