A practical AI-tool account reportedly reached about 22,000 followers by publishing Codex and Claude Code tutorials and prompt examples. One post reached 15,000 likes and more than 1.5 million views. Monetization came from a 19.9 RMB “Codex notes” product that sold 169 copies, plus paid remote-assistance work. The arithmetic turns the story from “viral tutorials” into a small product-to-service funnel.

## The disclosed funnel math

| Metric | Reported or derived |
|---|---:|
| Followers | 22,000 |
| Peak likes on one post | 15,000 |
| Peak views on one post | 1.5M+ |
| Notebook price | 19.9 RMB |
| Notebook sales | 169 |
| Notebook gross revenue | 3,363.10 RMB |
| Sales ÷ followers | 0.77% |
| Gross revenue per follower | 0.153 RMB |

The 0.77% ratio is not a clean conversion rate. Some buyers may not follow the account, many followers may never have seen the offer, and the follower count may have grown after the sales period. It is still a useful scale check. A 22,000-follower account did not turn a 19.9 RMB file into a large standalone business. The disclosed file revenue is 3,363.10 RMB before fees and support.

That does not make the file a bad product. It changes its job. The notebook can turn broad tutorial attention into a list of 169 people who paid for help with Codex. Those buyers are more valuable than anonymous viewers when the next offer is remote setup, debugging, workflow design, or team training.

## Context: tutorials attract problems, not only audiences

Codex and Claude Code content has a built-in commercial signal. A person watching a prompt demonstration may be curious. A person downloading notes, copying commands, and asking why the setup fails has an active implementation problem.

The account reportedly posted concrete tutorials and prompt cases rather than general AI news. That difference matters. General news can produce large reach with weak purchase intent. A tutorial about a specific task attracts fewer people but exposes a point where some viewers will pay to avoid configuration time or failure.

The content funnel has three layers:

1. **Free proof.** A short tutorial demonstrates that the operator can solve a narrow problem.
2. **Low-price compression.** The 19.9 RMB notebook packages commands, prompts, and procedures into a self-serve reference.
3. **Paid intervention.** Remote assistance handles environment issues, custom workflows, and cases the document cannot solve.

The first layer builds attention, the second records buyer intent, and the third can carry most of the margin. Without the third layer, the disclosed notebook would need far more volume to become meaningful income.

## What 19.9 RMB can and cannot support

At 19.9 RMB, support economics are unforgiving. If every buyer asks one ten-minute question, 169 buyers consume 28.2 hours. Gross notebook revenue per support hour would be about 119 RMB before fees, content creation, refunds, and tax. If average support reaches 30 minutes, the number falls below 40 RMB per hour.

The file therefore needs a hard boundary: fixed contents, clear update policy, no individualized debugging included. Remote help should be a separate paid offer.

A simple service scenario shows the potential difference:

| Service conversion from 169 buyers | Service orders | Price per order | Service revenue |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 3% | 5 | 299 RMB | 1,495 RMB |
| 5% | 8 | 499 RMB | 3,992 RMB |
| 10% | 17 | 999 RMB | 16,983 RMB |

These are scenarios, not reported results. They show why the missing remote-assistance number matters more than the 169-copy headline. At 5% conversion and 499 RMB per order, service revenue already exceeds notebook revenue. At 10% and 999 RMB, the service layer is roughly five times the notebook gross.

## Cost structure and unit economics

The digital file has near-zero delivery cost but not zero operating cost:

- **Content research.** Commands and interfaces change quickly; examples need re-testing.
- **Packaging.** Screenshots, structure, proofreading, and version labels take time.
- **Payment and platform fees.** Small-ticket products lose a noticeable share to fixed or percentage fees.
- **Refunds and piracy.** A copyable document can spread outside the paid channel.
- **Customer support.** Low-price buyers can still create high-touch questions.
- **Update obligation.** A product tied to fast-moving tools becomes stale quickly.

Suppose the platform and refunds consume 10% of gross revenue. Net before labor is about 3,026.79 RMB. If the notebook took 20 hours to create and update, the first 169 sales generated 151 RMB per production hour before customer support. That can be reasonable for a reusable asset, but it is not a large business by itself.

The content account also carries production cost. Researching, recording, editing, and replying may consume more hours than writing the notebook. Viral reach can disguise that labor because the platform does not show a cost line.

## The useful metric is not follower count

The account’s 22,000 followers and 1.5 million peak views show distribution. They do not show a dependable funnel. A better measurement chain is:

```text
Tutorial views
→ profile visits
→ product-page visits
→ completed notebook purchases
→ buyers requesting help
→ paid remote-assistance orders
→ repeat or referral work
```

Each step answers a different question. High views with low profile visits mean the content entertains but does not create intent. Product-page visits without purchases point to weak packaging or trust. Purchases without service enquiries can still be fine if the file is profitable, but they do not support a service model. Service orders without repeat work may mean the delivery is too narrow or inconsistent.

A tutorial account can also suffer audience mismatch. The posts that win the most likes may be beginner-friendly prompts, while the buyers who pay for remote assistance have complex local environments or company constraints. The operator has to decide whether content serves reach or qualifies the right problem.

## What this case does not cover

- **No total revenue.** Remote-assistance income is mentioned but not priced or counted.
- **No time window.** We do not know how long it took to reach 22,000 followers or sell 169 copies.
- **No traffic funnel.** Product-page visits, checkout completion, refunds, and support volume are absent.
- **No content volume.** “One strong post every one or two pieces” is a claim without a total sample.
- **No cost data.** Recording, editing, updates, payment fees, and customer support are not included.
- **No retention.** A one-off notebook and one-off remote session do not automatically create recurring revenue.
- **No product freshness policy.** Codex and Claude Code change quickly; outdated instructions can increase refunds and support.

The peak-like number is especially easy to overread. Fifteen thousand likes can come from a broad audience that never buys. The 169 paid transactions are the stronger commercial signal, even though they are much smaller.

## Bottom line

The disclosed product is a 3,363.10 RMB gross microproduct, not a large information business. Its strategic value lies in turning a tutorial audience into 169 identified buyers with a specific Codex problem. A paid remote-assistance layer can be worth more than the file if support is priced separately and the account attracts implementation intent rather than generic AI curiosity.

The next test is not another viral post. It is a measured funnel: one tutorial format, one notebook landing page, one clearly priced assistance offer, and a record of every step from view to paid service. That data will show whether the account owns a repeatable customer-acquisition channel or only a temporary attention spike.
