A case profiles a telehealth company launched by Matthew Gallagher in September 2024 with $20,000 of capital and zero employees. By end-2025: $400M in sales, 250,000 patients served. By 2026: on track for $1.8B. Total headcount: 2 — Gallagher and his brother. The interesting part is not the healthcare insight (what condition is being treated, what drug is being prescribed) but the structural one: Gallagher did not build a healthcare company. He built a customer-acquisition and experience layer on top of someone else's healthcare infrastructure. Doctors are subcontracted. Pharmacy is outsourced. Logistics is outsourced. Licenses are rented from a partner. The company itself owns three things: brand, distribution, customer relationship. The unit economics of a customer-acquisition layer are tech-grade, not healthcare-grade. Per-patient math, the asset-light structure, and four gaps the case does not address, below."

## The asset-light structure

```
Customer journey:
   Facebook / Google / TikTok ad
        ↓
   Telemedicine portal (owned)
        ↓
   Doctor consult (subcontracted to partner network)
        ↓
   Prescription written (subcontracted)
        ↓
   Pharmacy fulfillment (subcontracted)
        ↓
   Logistics (subcontracted, e.g. UPS / FedEx)
        ↓
   Customer support, billing, retention (owned)
        ↓
   Monthly subscription auto-renew (owned)
```

The owned components are: brand, performance marketing, customer portal, billing, customer support, retention. The subcontracted components are: doctor consults, prescription writing, pharmacy, logistics. The owned components are the customer-acquisition and customer-experience layer — a tech-grade business. The subcontracted components are the regulated healthcare work, paid for on a per-transaction basis.

## The capital efficiency

| Date | Capital | Sales run-rate | Employees | Capital turnover |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2024-09 | $20K | $0 | 0 | — |
| 2025-end | $20K | $400M | 2 | 20,000x |
| 2026 (projected) | $20K | $1.8B | 2 | 90,000x |

The capital turnover — $20K of starting capital into $400M of annualized sales — is the point. No traditional healthcare company can do this. The reason is the asset-light structure: the capital that would normally go to doctors, pharmacies, and logistics stays with the partner companies. Gallagher's company spends capital only on the customer-acquisition layer.

## Unit economics

```
Per-patient CAC:                  $50–$200  (performance marketing)
Per-patient LTV (12-month):       $1,500–$3,000  (subscription)
LTV/CAC ratio:                    7.5x–60x
Gross margin (post-partner pay):  40–70%  (typical telehealth customer-acq layer)
Net contribution margin:          15–30%  (after overhead, support, retention)
```

The case does not give per-patient numbers. The comparable numbers above are from public telehealth customer-acquisition layers in 2026. The central unit-economics variable is the gross margin. A 50% gross margin on $400M of sales gives $200M of contribution to overhead, which is software-grade. A 20% gross margin gives $80M, which is healthcare-grade. The case does not state the gross margin.

## Why the frame is 'customer acquisition', not 'healthcare'

What matters is the unit economics, not the regulatory status. A healthcare company is regulated-margin (5–15% gross, because the regulatory cost is internalized). A customer-acquisition layer is tech-margin (40–70% gross, because the regulatory cost is outsourced to the partner network). The same revenue line looks like two different businesses depending on which side of the structure you sit on.

The frame is not 'Gallagher runs a healthcare company'. The frame is 'Gallagher runs a customer-acquisition layer that happens to be in healthcare'. The two frames imply very different multiples. A healthcare-company frame implies a 1–3x revenue multiple. A customer-acquisition-layer frame implies a 3–8x revenue multiple.

## What the case does not cover

Four gaps.

1. **Gross margin.** The case gives revenue, not gross margin. The central unit-economics variable is unstated. A 50% gross and a 20% gross are different businesses.
2. **Churn.** Telehealth subscription churn typically runs 5–10%/month. The case does not give a renewal figure. At 10%/month, the company must acquire 25,000 new patients per month to grow at the current rate.
3. **Refund / chargeback rate.** Telehealth sees 3–8% chargeback rates from patients who feel the product did not work. At 250,000 patients, the implied chargeback volume is 7,500–20,000 cases, which is a meaningful operational burden and a meaningful revenue reversal.
4. **Compliance overhead.** The case frames 2 employees, but the partner companies absorb the compliance cost. That cost is real and is paid for out of the gross margin. The case does not state the partner-fee structure.

## Take-away

The case is not 'a 2-person company did $400M in healthcare'. The case is: an asset-light customer-acquisition layer in a regulated industry can run tech-grade unit economics, because the regulated work is outsourced to partners who charge per transaction. The owned layer is brand, distribution, and customer relationship. The subcontracted layer is the regulated work.

For most operators reading this case, the bottom line is: stop trying to build a healthcare company. Find a regulated industry where the customer-acquisition and customer-experience work is the bottleneck, build that layer, and subcontract the regulated work. The unit economics will be tech-grade, not industry-grade.
