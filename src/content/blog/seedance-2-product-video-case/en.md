faq:
  - q: 'How much revenue does the case describe?'
    a: 'Six-month revenue curve over months 1-6: month 1 around 2K RMB, month 2 around 5K, month 3 above 10K, month 6 around 30K. Self-reported numbers, not audit-verified.'
  - q: 'What does the international pricing look like?'
    a: 'Three Upwork tiers: entry 250-450 USD per 60-second product video, standard 600-1200 USD with script collaboration and dual-format delivery, premium 1500-3500 USD for a 3-5 video bundle with multilingual subtitles.'
  - q: 'Why does the author say "now" is the window?'
    a: 'Three reasons are given: (1) Seedance 2.0 reached "good enough" on 4K, character consistency and product-shape stability around Q2 2026; (2) most SMB brands still expect AI video to look fake, so the demo converts hard; (3) the supply of operators who can deliver commercial-grade output is thin, so pricing power sits with the seller side.'
  - q: 'What is the AI leverage point?'
    a: 'Traditional production chain: script -> storyboard -> shoot -> edit -> color -> subtitles -> delivery, 3-5 people, 2-4 weeks. AI chain: script (GPT) -> Seedance/Kling generation -> in-app subtitles -> delivery, 1 person, 1-3 days. Labor cost falls from tens of thousands of RMB to a few hundred RMB of subscription fees — that is a cost-structure rewrite, not an efficiency upgrade.'
  - q: 'What are the pitfalls the post warns about?'
    a: 'Product-shape consistency is the hardest. The example given is a thermos whose shape drifts between frames. The post suggests pinning key frames to no more than four per shot, treating the first frame as the quality anchor, and budgeting a learning curve of ~one full day per complex video before speed picks up.'
  - q: 'Is this a repeatable model?'
    a: 'Partially. The unit economics hold for product-video niches where the customer has weak taste discrimination and tight delivery deadlines (e-commerce listing, Amazon main image video, TikTok Shop item clip). It does not generalize to brand films, narrative shorts, or anything with on-screen talent — those still need human direction.'
