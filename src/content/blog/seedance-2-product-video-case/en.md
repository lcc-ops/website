A case from a Chinese knowledge-planet group describes a Hangzhou product designer whose monthly pay went from 8K RMB to 30K RMB within six months by switching from detail-page design to AI-generated product videos with Seedance 2.0. The post also quotes three international pricing tiers and a four-window argument for why the timing matters. Below is the cold-eyed breakdown — what is interesting, what is overstated, and what the math actually implies.

## The six-month revenue curve, as posted

| Month | Revenue (RMB) | Source notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | ~2,000 | "ten small gigs" |
| 2 | ~5,000 | "referrals from earlier clients" |
| 3 | ~10,000 | "one brand ordered a 20-video batch" |
| 6 | ~30,000 | "stable" (self-described) |

What the curve actually shows: this is the front half of a typical freelance hockey stick. Months 1-2 are loss-leader pricing (first gig was 200 RMB); month 3 onwards is volume, not rate; month 6 plus is when referrals compound. **The 30K figure is steady-state, not peak.** Treat it as the answer to "what does the plateau look like at this gig density," not "what can you earn at the top."

Numbers are self-reported. The post does not post invoices.

## The cost structure collapse

The interesting claim is not "AI video is good now" — it is the cost-structure rewrite:

```
Traditional chain: script → storyboard → shoot → edit → color → subtitles → delivery
                    3–5 people, 2–4 weeks

AI chain:          script (GPT) → Seedance/Kling → in-app subtitles → delivery
                    1 person, 1–3 days
```

This is the part that makes the case durable. Quality progress (the "is it good enough?" question) gets the press, but the unit economics flip because labor cost falls from tens of thousands of RMB per piece to a few hundred RMB of subscription fees. **At the SMB end of the market, customers buy on deadline and price, not on cinematic craft, so the rewrite is enough.**

## International pricing tiers (as quoted by the source)

| Tier | Deliverable | Price (USD) |
|---|---|---|
| Entry | 60-second product video, 3-day delivery | 250 – 450 |
| Standard | Script collaboration, brand customization, dual aspect ratio | 600 – 1,200 |
| Premium | 3–5 video bundle, multilingual subtitles | 1,500 – 3,500 |

The author's framing: "Delivery time is 1/5 of traditional, price is 1/3 of traditional, your effective hourly rate is 3x." The math holds **only** if the operator is billing by package, not by hour. Hourly billing kills the leverage because the buyer compares hours.

## The four-window argument — and where it strains

The post argues that "now" is the window for three reasons:
1. Tool quality threshold crossed (4K, character consistency, product-shape stability) around Q2 2026.
2. SMB buyer expectation still anchored on "AI video = obvious fake," so demos convert.
3. Operator supply is thin — pricing power sits with sellers.

All three are plausible, two are time-limited:
- (1) is real but flattens within 6-12 months as tools converge.
- (2) decays as the demo set circulates.
- (3) is the only one that creates durable margin, and it only protects the **first wave** of operators who build reputation.

Practical implication: the model pays best for the next 12 months. After that, the buyer education curve closes and someone on the platform will package the same service at half the price, with similar quality, because the supply curve catches up.

## What the post does NOT cover

- Taxes and platform fees. Upwork takes ~10-20% from freelancers. KDP-style marketplaces won't apply here, but Stripe + the platform cut is real.
- Refund and dispute rate. AI videos still fail client taste tests on a non-trivial share of jobs.
- Repeat purchase. The author describes referrals, but the post does not say what fraction of month 6 revenue is recurring vs one-off.
- The "30K steady state" is for one Hangzhou operator with a strong design background. A pure technical operator without taste will land 30-50% lower.

## Take-away

If you have a design background and want product videos as a side income: the case is internally consistent, the numbers are plausible, and the window is real. If you are a pure-prompt operator entering cold, plan for 3-4 months before month 3's number, not the month 1 trajectory the source post leads with.
