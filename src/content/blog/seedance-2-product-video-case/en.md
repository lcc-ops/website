A Hangzhou product designer switches from detail-page design to AI-generated product videos on Seedance 2.0 in January 2026. Monthly pay goes from 8K RMB to about 30K RMB within six months. The post also quotes three international pricing tiers and a four-window argument for why the timing matters. The math, plus what is overstated.

## The six-month revenue curve, as posted

| Month | Revenue (RMB) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | ~2,000 | ten small gigs |
| 2 | ~5,000 | referrals from earlier clients |
| 3 | ~10,000 | one brand ordered a 20-video batch |
| 6 | ~30,000 | "stable" (self-described) |

The curve reads as the front half of a typical freelance hockey stick. Months 1-2 are loss-leader pricing (the first gig was 200 RMB). From month 3 onwards volume matters more than rate. Month 6 plus is where referrals compound. **30K is steady-state, not peak.** Treat it as the answer to "what does the plateau look like at this gig density," not "what can you earn at the top."

Numbers are self-reported. The post does not post invoices.

## The cost structure collapse

The interesting claim is not "AI video is good enough now." It is the cost-structure rewrite:

```
Traditional chain: script -> storyboard -> shoot -> edit -> color -> subtitles -> delivery
                    3-5 people, 2-4 weeks

AI chain:          script (GPT) -> Seedance/Kling -> in-app subtitles -> delivery
                    1 person, 1-3 days
```

This is the part that makes the case durable. Quality progress gets the press; the unit economics flip because labor cost falls from tens of thousands of RMB per piece to a few hundred RMB of subscription. At the SMB end of the market, customers buy on deadline and price, not on cinematic craft. The rewrite is enough.

## International pricing tiers (as posted)

| Tier | Deliverable | Price (USD) |
|---|---|---|
| Entry | 60-second product video, 3-day delivery | 250 – 450 |
| Standard | Script collaboration, brand customization, dual aspect ratio | 600 – 1,200 |
| Premium | 3-5 video bundle, multilingual subtitles | 1,500 – 3,500 |

The framing: "delivery time is 1/5 of traditional, price is 1/3 of traditional, your effective hourly rate is 3x." The math holds **only** if the operator bills by package, not by hour. Hourly billing kills the leverage because the buyer compares hours.

## The four-window argument, and where it strains

The post argues "now" is the window for three reasons:
1. Tool quality threshold crossed (4K, character consistency, product-shape stability) around Q2 2026.
2. SMB buyer expectation still anchored on "AI video = obvious fake," so demos convert.
3. Operator supply is thin; pricing power sits with sellers.

All three are plausible; two are time-limited:
- (1) is real but flattens within 6-12 months as tools converge.
- (2) decays as the demo set circulates.
- (3) is the only one that creates durable margin, and it only protects the **first wave** of operators who build reputation.

Practical implication: the model pays best for the next 12 months. After that, the buyer education curve closes and someone on the platform will package the same service at half the price with similar quality, because the supply curve catches up.

## What the post does NOT cover

- Taxes and platform fees. Upwork takes ~10-20% from freelancers. Stripe + the platform cut is real.
- Refund and dispute rate. AI videos still fail client taste tests on a non-trivial share of jobs.
- Repeat purchase. The post describes referrals, but does not say what fraction of month 6 revenue is recurring vs one-off.
- The "30K steady state" is for one Hangzhou operator with a strong design background. A pure technical operator without taste lands 30-50% lower.

## Take-away

Design background plus product videos as a side income: the case is internally consistent, the numbers plausible, the window real. Pure-prompt operator entering cold: plan for 3-4 months before month 3's number, not the month 1 trajectory the post leads with.
